This document describes a threat model for the Recognized Entities specification. It identifies and analyzes security and privacy threats specific to ecosystems that use RecognizedEntityCredential resources to express recognition of entities and the actions they are authorized to perform, such as issuing or verifying verifiable credentials.

This threat model is a work in progress. It is published alongside the Recognized Entities specification to inform implementers and ecosystem designers of the security and privacy considerations relevant to deployments of the specification. Feedback and proposed additions are welcome via the issue tracker.

Introduction

Threat modeling is a vital part of specification development. This document provides a threat model for the Recognized Entities specification, which defines a data model for expressing that one or more entities are recognized to perform specific actions — such as issuing or verifying verifiable credentials — within a particular ecosystem.

This threat model follows the methodology described in the W3C Threat Modeling Guide and uses the STRIDE taxonomy for classifying threats. Threats are grouped into four categories:

  • Target Threats — threats the specification was designed to address.
  • Implementation Threats — threats that implementers must handle; the specification leaves the choice of mitigation open.
  • External Threats — real-world risks acknowledged as out of scope for this version of the specification.
  • Dependency Threats — threats inherited from technologies the specification depends upon.

Description

The Recognized Entities ecosystem enables a list publisher — such as a national business register, an accreditation authority, or an industry consortium — to issue a RecognizedEntityCredential asserting that one or more entities are recognized to perform specific actions. A holder can acquire such a credential and present it to a verifier alongside their own verifiable credential, allowing the verifier to confirm both the integrity of the presented credential and the recognized status of its issuer.

The core trust challenge is that a verifier receiving a credential from an unknown issuer must be able to determine, in an automated and cryptographically-verifiable way, whether that issuer is recognized within the relevant ecosystem. The Recognized Entities specification addresses this by providing a data model for publishing recognition information as a verifiable credential, enabling a chain of trust from verifier trust anchors through list publishers to individual recognized entities.

For this threat model, we consider a minimal instantiation of the Recognized Entities ecosystem consisting of five roles: the List Publisher who issues recognized entity credentials, the Recognized Entity / Issuer who receives recognition and issues verifiable credentials, the Holder who receives and presents those credentials, the Verifier who verifies the credential chain, and the Governance Authority who sets the policies under which recognition is granted.

Note: We do NOT model potential attackers as first-class elements, as over-characterizing attackers can lead to analysis bias that is better avoided.

Data Flow Diagram for the Recognized Entities Ecosystem

The data flow diagram below illustrates the key elements and data flows in a minimal Recognized Entities ecosystem. The diagram shows how a recognized entity credential is issued by a list publisher to a recognized entity, how the holder presents that credential alongside a verifiable credential to a verifier, and how the verifier resolves the chain of trust back to its configured trust anchors.

The following notation is used in the diagram description and dictionary:

  • E — External Entity (human or legal person outside the system boundary)
  • P — Process (running algorithm that transforms data)
  • F — Data Flow (movement of data between elements)
  • S — Data Store (persistent or ephemeral storage)
  • O — Data Object (self-contained, transferable artifact)
  • C — Container (collection of elements under a single authority)

Dictionary

E1 Recognized Entity / Issuer An entity — such as a university, a trade exporter, or a conformity assessment body — that has been granted a RecognizedEntityCredential by a list publisher and uses it to issue verifiable credentials to holders. This entity controls a DID whose identifier appears as the credentialSubject.id of the recognized entity credential and as the issuer of the verifiable credentials it produces.
E2 Holder A person or organization that receives a verifiable credential from a recognized entity and presents it — together with the recognized entity credential — to a verifier. The holder stores credentials in a digital wallet and controls what is disclosed to verifiers.
E3 Verifier An entity — such as a customs authority, a trade finance lender, or a market surveillance authority — that receives a verifiable credential from a holder and must determine whether the issuer of that credential is recognized within the relevant ecosystem. The verifier is configured with one or more trust anchors that identify the list publishers it considers authoritative.
E4 List Publisher An entity — such as a national business register, an accreditation authority, or an industry consortium — that issues RecognizedEntityCredential resources asserting that one or more entities are recognized to perform specific actions. The list operator is the root of trust for any recognized entity credentials it issues.
E5 Governance Authority A legal person or consortium that sets the policies, criteria, and rules under which a list publisher may grant recognition. Examples include government regulators, standards bodies, and industry governance frameworks. The governance authority may or may not be the same entity as the list operator.
P1 Credential Issuance Process The running algorithm by which a recognized entity signs and issues a verifiable credential to a holder. This process uses the recognized entity's private key material to produce a cryptographic proof over the credential.
P2 Credential Verification Process The running algorithm by which a verifier checks the cryptographic proof of a presented verifiable credential, resolves the issuer's recognized entity credential, and confirms that the issuer is recognized within a trusted list. This process may involve fetching recognized entity credentials from external URLs or from holder-provided presentations.
P3 List Publication Process The running algorithm by which a list publisher creates, signs, and publishes a recognized entity credential. This process applies the operator's private key material to produce a cryptographic proof, then makes the credential available at a URL or via a DID-linked resource.
F1 VC Issuance The data flow by which a recognized entity delivers a signed verifiable credential to a holder. The credential contains the recognized entity's DID as its issuer value.
F2 Credential Presentation The data flow by which a holder presents a verifiable credential — and optionally a recognized entity credential — to a verifier. This flow crosses a trust boundary between the holder's wallet and the verifier's verification process.
F3 List Fetch The data flow by which a verifier retrieves a recognized entity credential directly from the list publisher's server, either to confirm an issuer's current status or to obtain a more recent version of the credential than the one provided by the holder.
F4 RecognizedEntityCredential Issuance The data flow by which a list publisher delivers a signed RecognizedEntityCredential to a recognized entity. This credential asserts that the entity's DID is recognized to perform one or more specific actions.
S1 Holder Credential Store The persistent storage within the holder's wallet where verifiable credentials and recognized entity credentials are kept. In the holder's trust container, this storage is accessible only to the holder's wallet software.
S2 List Data Store The persistent storage within the list publisher's server where recognized entity credentials are kept and made available for retrieval. This store is managed by the list publisher and is typically accessible to the public via HTTPS.
O1 Verifiable Credential A cryptographically-signed data object issued by a recognized entity to a holder. The credential asserts claims about the holder and is signed with the recognized entity's private key, allowing any party to verify its integrity and the identity of its issuer.
O2 RecognizedEntityCredential A cryptographically-signed data object issued by a list publisher to a recognized entity. It asserts that the entity's DID is recognized to perform one or more actions — such as issuing or verifying specific types of verifiable credentials — within the ecosystem governed by the list operator.
C1 Holder Wallet The container — typically a mobile or desktop application — that manages the holder's credentials, keys, and DID. All credential storage (S1) and presentation logic runs within this container, which is under the holder's control.
C2 List Server The container — typically a web server or DID-linked resource host — managed by the list publisher to store (S2) and serve recognized entity credentials. It runs the list publication process (P3) and responds to list fetch requests (F3).

Stakeholders

E1 Recognized Entity / Issuer

A recognized entity is any person or organization that has been granted a RecognizedEntityCredential by a list publisher and uses that recognition to issue verifiable credentials to holders. Examples include universities issuing academic credentials, national business registers issuing trade entity credentials, and conformity assessment bodies issuing product certificates.

The recognized entity's primary goal is to issue verifiable credentials that will be accepted by verifiers within the relevant ecosystem. They want confidence that their recognized status is accurately represented, that their recognition credentials are not misappropriated or tampered with, and that their DID remains under their control.

E2 Holder

A holder is a person or organization that receives verifiable credentials from recognized entities and presents them to verifiers. In the cross-border trade use case, the holder is an importer presenting an exporter's invoice credential to customs. In the education use case, the holder is a student presenting a university-issued diploma.

The holder's primary goal is to present credentials that will be accepted by verifiers, without disclosing more information than necessary. Holders have a strong interest in privacy: they do not want their credential presentations to leak information about their activities to list operators or other third parties.

E3 Verifier

A verifier is an entity that receives a credential presentation from a holder and must determine whether to accept it. Verifiers range from automated customs clearance systems processing thousands of trade documents per hour to human-operated market surveillance authorities checking product conformity certificates.

The verifier's primary goal is to confirm that the credential it has received was issued by an entity that is genuinely recognized within the relevant ecosystem, and that the credential itself has not been tampered with. Verifiers also have a strong interest in availability: a denial of service against the lists they depend on directly impairs their ability to function.

E4 List Publisher

A list publisher is an entity that issues RecognizedEntityCredential resources. List operators are the root of trust for any recognized entity credentials they issue. They range from national government bodies (such as business registers and accreditation authorities) to industry consortia and standards organizations.

List operators have a strong interest in ensuring that only properly vetted entities appear in their lists, and that their list credentials are not forged or misused. They also bear operational responsibility for the availability and integrity of the credentials they publish.

E5 Governance Authority

Governance authorities set the rules under which list publishers may grant recognition. They may be government regulators, international standards bodies, or industry governance frameworks. In some ecosystems the governance authority and the list publisher are the same entity; in others they are distinct.

Governance authorities have an interest in preventing anti-competitive gatekeeping, ensuring that recognition criteria are applied consistently and transparently, and that the ecosystem as a whole remains open to legitimate new entrants. They may also have law-enforcement interests in being able to revoke the recognition of bad actors.

Contributing

This threat model is maintained alongside the Recognized Entities specification. Feedback, bug reports, and proposed additions are welcome via the issue tracker.

Adding a Threat

To add a new threat to the model, provide the following components:

  1. A short, descriptive name
  2. A unique ID (letter T followed by an integer, e.g., T6)
  3. A description of the threat with enough detail for readers to understand its scope
  4. One or more responses, each with a name, unique ID (letter R followed by an integer), response type (Eliminate, Reduce, Transfer, or Accept), and description
  5. A list of affected DFD elements drawn from the dictionary above
  6. The threat taxonomy used (e.g., STRIDE) and the threat class within that taxonomy

Create a JavaScript file following the pattern of the existing threat files, register it with window.ThreatModel.register(threat), add a <script> tag in index.html, and add the threat ID to the appropriate category in threats/outline.js.
